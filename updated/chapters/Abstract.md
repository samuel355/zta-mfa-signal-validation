# Abstract

The widespread adoption of remote and hybrid work has exposed critical limitations in perimeter-based security. Zero Trust Architecture (ZTA) and Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) strengthen access control, but their effectiveness is undermined by unreliable contextual signals, siloed Security Information and Event Management (SIEM) systems, usability challenges, and inadequate privacy safeguards.

This study proposes and evaluates a multi-source context validation framework that strengthens Zero Trust MFA by cross-verifying contextual signals (GPS, IP, Wi-Fi BSSID, device posture, and TLS fingerprint) before incorporating them into authentication risk decisions, and, following a mid-study extension, by adding real network-flow-based detection of Denial-of-Service and Elevation-of-Privilege activity. The framework was implemented as containerised microservices and evaluated against an ablation configuration (validation layer disabled) and two re-implemented published baselines — Ahmadi (2025) and Phani Kumar Kanuri (2025) — on a shared, disclosed dataset combining CIC-IDS2018 network-attack traffic with real production login data from the RBA (Risk-Based Authentication) dataset.

Final review surfaced four distinct defects, each corrected in turn: a target-leakage bug in which the proposed framework's risk score had been computed in part from the same ground-truth attack label later used to grade its own decisions; a decision threshold left calibrated against the resulting inflated risk distribution; a data-generation bug in which the session generator relabelled real dataset rows with a synthetic attack category chosen independently of the row's actual content; and, once the first three were fixed and the framework's real detection scope became visible, two genuinely missing detection capabilities that real, non-label network-flow signals — calibrated directly against measured CIC-IDS2018 Benign-versus-attack statistics — turned out to support. The results reported here reflect all four corrections applied together on one final, fully re-collected dataset (n = 1,999 sessions).

The corrected proposed framework achieves 17.29% TPR at 2.77% FPR (96.18% precision, F1 = 0.293, AUC = 0.720 — the highest AUC of the four configurations compared). McNemar's test shows the proposed framework is statistically indistinguishable from Ahmadi (2025) (p = 0.705) — a genuine tie with the stronger published baseline reproduction — while significantly outperforming both Phani (2025) (p < 10⁻¹⁰) and the no-validation ablation configuration (p < 10⁻⁵³), the latter comparison being the clearest evidence that the validation layer itself contributes real, statistically significant detection capability. The framework now leads on four of six STRIDE categories (Elevation of Privilege, Repudiation, Denial of Service, and the ablation comparison throughout); Ahmadi (2025) retains a clear lead on Spoofing and Tampering. Information Disclosure remains undetected by every configuration tested — for the proposed framework this is reported as a tested, evidence-backed negative result (real Benign-versus-Infiltration flow statistics show no separating signal at any threshold examined) rather than an unexplored gap. Median decision latency is 50ms, at the edge of this study's originally hypothesised bound; p95/p99 latency (2.4s/3.1s) is dominated by synchronous Elasticsearch writes in the current deployment. Adding the two new detection signals introduced a small, disclosed usability cost (2.77% combined step-up/deny rate on legitimate sessions, versus a strict zero before their addition). Privacy-preserving mechanisms were specified as design requirements but not verified as implemented in the running service, and are reported as such rather than as a measured result. A 17.29% true-positive rate means the framework misses most attacks in this dataset; it is not proposed as a sole access-control gate, and a defensible deployment model — a high-precision, low-friction risk signal feeding a broader adaptive-MFA policy alongside other controls, rather than replacing them — is set out as future work rather than claimed as a current capability.

This study's contribution is a reproducible signal-quality architecture (`Qs = Fs × Cs × Es`) extended with genuinely new, empirically-calibrated network-flow detection, evaluated through a controlled and disclosed head-to-head comparison against published baselines' otherwise unverifiable self-reported results — and a documented account of its own multi-stage revision history, offered as a concrete demonstration of why target-leakage and data-integrity discipline are necessary in adaptive-authentication evaluation specifically, not just in supervised ML generally.

**Keywords:** Zero Trust Architecture (ZTA); Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA); Contextual Signal Validation; Network-Flow Detection; SIEM Integration; STRIDE Threat Mapping; Quality Scoring; Target Leakage; Adaptive MFA.
